Performance Reflection

What Worked Well

The headline stat this month is efficiency. January: 87 calls, $62k, $712/call. February: 48 calls, $81k, $1,695/call. Less than half the volume — 31% more revenue. That doesn't happen by accident. It's the result of compounding improvements across show rate, discovery quality, and offer delivery.

Show rate jumped from 59% to 78.7% — nearly 20 points. Better lead quality, tighter pre-call framing, fewer no-shows burning the calendar.

The close rate of 34.7% across all live calls since October reflects consistent performance across all call types and lead sources. The work on discovery — understanding the prospect's situation fully before presenting a solution — is showing up directly in the numbers. Prospects are sold before the price comes out.

The standout call of the month was Daniel Fitzgerald. Ian stayed patient, stayed curious, never pushed, and when the prospect signaled he wanted DFY over a course, Ian pivoted cleanly and closed. Mason flagged it for the Winning Call Vault as a model performance. That call is the template.

February also happened during a relocation. Selling a vehicle, travel logistics, adjusting to a new environment — and Ian posted his best efficiency month. That speaks to the baseline level of professionalism being built.

What Did Not Work Well

Volume was the constraint. 48 live calls is solid — but with $1,695/call, every extra slot is worth $1,695. January had 80+ scheduled calls. The months where volume meets efficiency are where $100k+ lives. Travel ate into availability, and that's understandable — but the opportunity cost is clear.

The Stefan S. call exposed a pattern that still needs to be eliminated: following the prospect down rabbit holes. When a call loses structure, there's a tendency to go with it rather than using a pivot question to regain control. The "ideal outcome" question exists specifically for this moment. It wasn't used, and the call dragged without ever finding a clear pain point to pitch against.

Discovery still comes too early to the pitch. The early-month roleplay with Mason revealed a tendency to move to the solution before pain is fully established. Closed-ended questions box prospects in and shut them down. The Situation → Problem → Data → Rationale framework is the right structure — but it requires consistent reps to become automatic under live pressure.

Proactive upselling was missed. Insiders clients from November–December were sitting at the 30–60 day mark — the exact window of maximum receptivity to a DFY upsell — with no systematic outreach. That's existing revenue left uncollected.

What Did You Learn

🧠 Pain Before Price
A prospect who hasn't articulated their own problem doesn't feel urgency. A prospect who has — in their own words — is already half-convinced.

The Situation → Problem → Data → Rationale framework isn't optional. It's the architecture of every clean close.
💬 "Two-Truths" Questioning
After affirming what's working, ask: "What would you want to improve about your lead gen if you could?" The prospect answers without ego and tells you exactly what to pitch. No confrontation. No hunting. They hand it to you.
🎯 Rational Questions Create Doubt
Challenge the status quo without pressure: "Why not just double down on conferences since they're working?" The prospect is forced to articulate the ceiling of their own strategy. They sell themselves on needing something different.
📊 Quantify the Gap
Turn vague desires into specific numbers: "How many more leads would you need per month to feel ready to hire a salesperson?" A specific number creates a measurable gap that Ian's service can fill precisely.
🎭 Tone Is a Weapon
The Daniel Fitzgerald call proved it. Calm, curious, non-reactive energy disarms resistance better than any technique.

Mason flagged it for the Winning Call Vault — not because Ian said the right things, but because of how he said everything.
💰 The 30–60 Day Upsell
Implement a check-in text for all new Insiders clients at the 30–60 day mark to proactively offer the DFY build.

Don't wait for them to come back. The money is already in the pipeline — go get it.
